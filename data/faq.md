## General Knowledge

### What is Judo?
Judo is a Japanese martial art and Olympic sport focused on throwing your opponent to the ground, pinning them, or submitting them with a choke or armlock. 

### Who invented Judo?
Judo was founded by Jigoro Kano in 1882. He adapted dangerous, ancient jujutsu techniques into a safe, educational, and highly effective sport.

### What does the word "Judo" mean?
Judo translates to "The Gentle Way." This refers to the principle of using an opponent's energy, weight, and momentum against them, rather than fighting force with force.

### What are the two core philosophies of Judo?
Jigoro Kano established two main maxims: 
1. **Seiryoku Zenyo:** Maximum efficiency with minimum effort.
2. **Jita Kyoei:** Mutual welfare and benefit (helping each other grow).

### Is Judo good for self-defense?
Yes. Judo teaches you how to control distance, stay balanced, take an attacker to the ground forcefully, and control them once there. These skills are highly effective in real-world scenarios.

### Will Judo help me lose weight and get in shape?
Absolutely. Judo is a full-body workout that combines heavy cardiovascular endurance with explosive strength training. It burns a massive amount of calories while building core and grip strength.

### What mental benefits does Judo provide?
Judo builds extreme resilience, discipline, and respect. It teaches you how to overcome the fear of physical confrontation, how to stay calm under pressure, and how to accept failure (getting thrown) and immediately get back up.

## Gear and Uniform

### What should I wear to my first class?
If you don't have a Judogi (uniform) yet, wear comfortable, athletic clothing with no zippers, buttons, or pockets (like sweatpants and a t-shirt).

### What is the difference between a Judo gi and a BJJ gi?
A Judo gi is heavier, looser fitting, and has wider sleeves to allow for the extensive gripping required. BJJ gis are tighter to prevent opponents from easily grabbing the fabric.

### Why do Judokas wear blue and white gis in competition?
In local dojos, everyone typically wears a white gi. In tournaments, one competitor wears blue and the other wears white so that the referees, judges, and TV audiences can easily tell them apart during fast scrambles.

### How often should I wash my Judogi?
You must wash your gi after every single practice. Never train in a dirty or smelly gi. It is a sign of disrespect and spreads skin infections.

### Should I wash my belt?
Yes. The old myth that washing your belt "washes away the knowledge" is false. Wash your belt regularly to keep it clean and hygienic.

### How do I tie my belt?
Find the center of the belt, place it on your stomach, wrap it around your back, bring the ends to the front, cross them, tuck the top end under both layers, and tie a square knot. (Your coach will show you on day one).

## Belt Ranks and Demographics

### What do the belt colors mean?
Belts indicate your rank and experience level. Beginners wear colored belts (Kyu grades) starting at white and moving up (often yellow, orange, green, blue, brown). Black belts (Dan grades) represent advanced practitioners who have mastered the fundamentals.

### How long does it take to get a black belt?
On average, for an adult practicing consistently (2-3 times a week), it takes about 4 to 6 years to earn a black belt (Shodan). A black belt simply means you are no longer a beginner.

### What are the red-and-white and solid red belts?
These are high-ranking black belts. A red-and-white paneled belt represents a 6th to 8th-degree black belt. A solid red belt represents a 9th or 10th-degree black belt, reserved for the absolute masters of the sport.

### At what age should kids start Judo?
Children can start as young as 4 or 5 years old. Kids' classes focus heavily on gymnastics, tumbling, falling safely (ukemi), and discipline, rather than aggressive competition.

### Is Judo safe for older adults to start?
Yes, but you must find a dojo that caters to recreational adults. Older beginners should spend extended time learning to fall safely and communicate openly with partners about avoiding overly aggressive sparring.

### Are men and women trained differently?
No. Judo techniques rely on leverage, not brute strength, making them identical for both men and women. Classes are mixed, though tournaments are separated by gender and weight class.

## Etiquette and Culture

### Why do we bow in Judo?
Bowing (Rei) is a sign of respect and gratitude. You bow to the mat to respect the training space, to the instructor for their teaching, and to your partner to thank them for keeping you safe.

### Do I have to call the instructor "Sensei"?
Yes, it is standard etiquette in Judo to address the head instructor as "Sensei," which translates to "teacher" or "one who has gone before."

### Can I wear jewelry on the mat?
No. All jewelry, including rings, necklaces, piercings, and watches, must be removed before stepping on the mat. They are severe injury hazards to both you and your partner.

### Why do I need to wear footwear off the mat?
To keep the training mats clean. You must wear sandals or shoes everywhere outside the dojo (especially the bathroom) and step barefoot onto the mat to prevent spreading bacteria like ringworm.

## Training and Safety

### Will Judo hurt?
You will experience muscle soreness and the physical impact of being thrown. However, once you learn proper breakfalls (Ukemi), the throws stop hurting. Severe injuries are rare if you train smartly.

### What is Ukemi?
Ukemi means "breakfalls." It is the system of falling safely to dissipate the energy of a throw and protect your head, neck, and joints. 

### Why do I need to learn to fall before I learn to throw?
If you cannot fall safely, you cannot practice safely. Learning to fall removes the fear of being thrown, allowing you to relax and actually learn the sport.

### What does a typical Judo class look like?
Most classes follow a strict structure:
1. Warm-up and Ukemi (falling practice).
2. Uchikomi (repetitive technique drilling).
3. Throwing practice on crash mats (Nagekomi).
4. Randori (live sparring).
5. Cool down and final bow.

### What should I do if I feel dizzy, exhausted, or hurt during practice?
Stop immediately. Step off the mat, let your partner know, and inform the coach. Never push through an injury or severe dizziness.

### How do I signal that a choke or armbar is working?
Tap loudly on your partner's body, tap the mat, or shout "Maitta!" (I give up). Tap early—do not wait until it hurts.

## Comparisons With Other Sports

### How is Judo different from Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ)?
Judo focuses primarily on standing throws (Tachi-waza) to achieve a decisive victory, with quick transitions to groundwork. BJJ focuses heavily on fighting on the ground (Ne-waza) to secure submissions. 

### How is Judo different from Olympic Wrestling?
Wrestling does not use a uniform (Gi), so grips are placed directly on the body. Furthermore, in Judo, throwing your opponent flat onto their back ends the match instantly (Ippon). In wrestling, you must hold the opponent's shoulders to the mat (a pin) to win decisively.

### Can Judo be used without a Gi (in street clothes)?
Yes. While Judo is trained in a Gi, the principles of leverage and off-balancing remain identical. Grips can easily be adapted to grab a winter coat, a shirt, or directly control the neck and wrists (underhooks and overhooks).

## Techniques and Mechanics

### What is Kuzushi?
Kuzushi is the Japanese term for off-balancing. It is the act of pulling or pushing your opponent to break their posture so they cannot defend your throw.

### Why do my throws feel so heavy and difficult?
If a throw requires all your muscle, you have no Kuzushi. You are trying to lift a balanced opponent. Break their balance first, and the throw will feel effortless.

### Can I grab my opponent's legs during a standing throw?
No. Modern international Judo rules strictly prohibit grabbing the opponent's legs or pants with your hands while standing. 

### Are strikes allowed in Judo?
No. Punching, kicking, and striking of any kind are strictly forbidden in competitive Judo and Randori.

## Rules and Watching the Sport

### Are there weight classes in Judo?
Yes. To ensure fair competition, athletes compete in strictly regulated weight categories (e.g., -60kg, -73kg, +100kg for men; -48kg, -57kg, +78kg for women).

### What is an Ippon?
An Ippon is a full point and ends the match immediately. It is scored by a fast, forceful throw landing the opponent on their back, a 20-second pin, or a submission.
* **Referee Signal:** The referee raises one arm straight up toward the ceiling.

### What is a Waza-ari?
A Waza-ari is a half-point. It is awarded for a throw that is not quite an Ippon or a pin held for 10 to 19 seconds. Two Waza-aris equal an Ippon and end the match.
* **Referee Signal:** The referee raises one arm out horizontally to the side.

### What happens if the match ends in a tie?
The match goes into "Golden Score," which is a sudden-death overtime. The clock resets, and the first person to score any point, or receive a disqualifying penalty, loses.

### What is a Shido?
A Shido is a minor penalty given for infractions like stalling, stepping out of bounds, or avoiding grips. Accumulating three Shidos results in disqualification.
* **Referee Signal:** The referee points at the penalized fighter and holds up a finger indicating the penalty.
