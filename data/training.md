# Training

## Warm-up
A proper Judo warm-up is critical to prepare your body for heavy physical demands and the impact of falling. 
* Start with light cardiovascular activity like jogging or skipping to raise your core temperature. 
* Move into dynamic stretching, focusing on your neck, shoulders, and hips.
* Finish every warm-up with extensive **Ukemi** (breakfall) practice. Falling safely is your primary defense against injury.

## Grip Strength
In Judo, whoever controls the grips controls the fight. Developing immense hand and forearm strength is essential for both attacking and defending.
* **Gi Pull-ups:** Hang an old gi jacket over a pull-up bar and grip the lapels or sleeves to perform pull-ups.
* **Dead Hangs:** Hang from a bar for time to build sustained grip endurance.
* **Farmer's Carries:** Walk while holding heavy kettlebells or dumbbells to build functional forearm strength.

## Mobility
Judo requires you to move your hips quickly and smoothly in all directions. You need mobility for both standing techniques (Tachi-waza) and groundwork (Ne-waza).
* Practice **Ebi** (shrimping or hip escapes) to build fundamental ground mobility.
* Practice bridging to protect your back and escape pins.
* Do forward and backward rolls to keep your spine agile and practice spatial awareness.

## Flexibility
While mobility is about active movement, flexibility helps your joints handle the extreme ranges of motion they will encounter during throws and submissions. 
* Dedicate time at the end of practice, when your muscles are fully warm, for static stretching. 
* Focus heavily on your shoulders, hips, and hamstrings, as these areas take the most stress during Judo.

## Conditioning
A Judo match consists of high-intensity, anaerobic bursts while another person tries to throw you. Traditional long-distance jogging is not enough.
* **Interval Training:** Mimic the pace of a match. Do 30 seconds of maximum effort (like sprinting or throwing heavy bags) followed by 10 to 15 seconds of rest.
* **Circuit Training:** Combine explosive movements (such as box jumps or kettlebell swings) with heavy pulling exercises to build combat-specific cardio.

## Recovery
You do not get stronger on the tatami (mat); you get stronger when you recover off the mat.
* **Sleep:** Aim for 7 to 9 hours a night to allow your central nervous system to recover from the intense gripping and pulling.
* **Nutrition:** Consume protein and carbohydrates shortly after training to repair muscle tissue and restore your energy reserves.
* **Active Recovery:** Do light walking, swimming, or cycling on your rest days to help flush lactic acid and reduce soreness.

## Randori
Randori is free practice or live sparring. It is where you pressure-test your techniques against a fully resisting opponent.
* **Learn, Do Not Compete:** Do not treat every Randori session like a gold medal match. 
* **Take Risks:** Try new techniques, open up your movement, and accept being thrown. 
* **Stay Loose:** Move lightly on your feet and avoid stiff-arming your partner away. If you are totally stiff, you are not learning Judo.

## Uchikomi
Uchikomi refers to repetitive drill training where you enter the throw but stop just before the execution phase. 
* This builds muscle memory, entry speed, and precision. 
* Focus entirely on your pulling action (kuzushi) and proper foot placement. 
* Do not rush. Build perfect mechanics first, then gradually add speed.

## Nagekomi
Nagekomi is repetitive throwing practice, usually done onto a thick crash mat. 
* This allows you to practice the full follow-through, power, and rotation of a technique without excessively battering your training partner. 
* Focus on finishing the throw cleanly, pulling up on the sleeve at the end to support your partner, and maintaining your own balance.

### Beginner Training Advice
* **Master Ukemi:** Never try to post an arm to stop a fall. Learn to take the breakfall perfectly. It will save you from severe injury and give you the confidence to attack.
* **Relax Your Arms:** Stiff, locked arms drain your energy rapidly and telegraph exactly what you are trying to do. Keep your grips relaxed until the exact second you attack.
* **Do Not Force It with Muscle:** If a throw feels incredibly heavy and requires all your strength, your mechanics are wrong. Stop forcing it, reset, and check your positioning.
* **Tap Early and Often:** In groundwork (Ne-waza), if you are caught in a choke or joint lock, tap your partner or the mat immediately. There is no pride in getting put to sleep or tearing a joint in practice.
* **Maintain Hygiene:** Always wash your gi after every single practice. Keep your fingernails and toenails trimmed short to avoid cutting your training partners.
