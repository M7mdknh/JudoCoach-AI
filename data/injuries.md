# Injuries

## Common Injuries
Judo is a high-impact contact sport. While safe when practiced correctly, certain injuries are common.
*   **Shoulders (AC Joint & Dislocations):** Often caused by falling directly on the point of the shoulder or attempting to post an arm to stop a throw.
*   **Knees (ACL/MCL Sprains):** Usually occur when a foot is planted flat on the mat and the body twists forcefully, often during botched leg sweeps or defensive turning.
*   **Fingers and Wrists:** Sprains and joint inflammation are incredibly common due to the intense, continuous gripping of the thick judogi material.
*   **Toes ("Mat Toe"):** Spraining the toes by catching them on the tatami (mat) during rapid footwork or shots.
*   **Neck Strains:** Caused by resisting throws with the head or taking awkward falls.

## Prevention
The best way to handle an injury is to never get it in the first place.
*   **Master Ukemi:** Breakfalls are your armor. Ninety percent of impact injuries happen because a judoka forgot how to fall or tried to stop a throw with their arm.
*   **Strength Training:** Build armor around your joints. Lifting weights, especially targeting the hamstrings, shoulders, and back, stabilizes your body against sudden forces.
*   **Tape Your Fingers:** If your knuckles ache, tape them before practice to provide structural support to the joints.
*   **Relax Your Grips:** A death grip fatigues your forearms and destroys your finger joints over time. Grip with just enough force to maintain control.

## Warm-up Importance
Never skip the warm-up. Walking onto the mat cold is an invitation for muscle tears and joint sprains.
*   **Physical Readiness:** A warm-up literally raises the temperature of your muscles and synovial fluid in your joints, making them more elastic and capable of absorbing shock.
*   **Mental Readiness:** Judo requires intense focus. The warm-up transitions your mind from the outside world to the dojo, improving reaction time and spatial awareness.
*   **Ukemi First:** Your warm-up must always include a progressive series of breakfalls before you ever take a live throw.

## Recovery
How you treat your body off the mat determines how long you survive on the mat.
*   **Immediate Care (R.I.C.E.):** For acute, fresh injuries, use Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation to manage swelling in the first 48 hours.
*   **Active Recovery:** Once swelling subsides, promote blood flow to the injured area with light movement, heat, and mobility exercises.
*   **Physical Therapy:** Do not just wait for the pain to go away. See a professional to rebuild the strength and stability of an injured joint.

## When to Stop Training
There is a massive difference between being muscle-sore and being injured. Know when to step off the mat.
*   **Sharp, Shooting Pain:** If a movement causes sudden, sharp pain, stop immediately.
*   **Pops and Snaps:** If you hear or feel a loud pop in a joint (knee, shoulder, elbow), do not test it. Sit down and ice it.
*   **Head Impacts:** If you hit your head and feel dizzy, nauseous, or have a headache, you are done for the day. Never mess with concussions.
*   **Compensating:** If an injury forces you to limp or completely change your mechanics to avoid pain, stop. You are just going to injure something else.

## Safety Tips
*   **Do Not Post:** Never reach out with a stiff, straight arm to catch yourself when falling. You will snap your collarbone, dislocate your shoulder, or break your elbow. Take the fall.
*   **Tap Early:** In Ne-waza (groundwork), if an armbar or choke is locked in, tap instantly. Practicing your pain tolerance will only get you surgery.
*   **Communicate:** If you have a tweaked knee or shoulder, tell your training partners before Randori begins so they know what to avoid.
*   **Trim Your Nails:** Keep your fingernails and toenails incredibly short. Long nails will slice open your training partners during gripping exchanges.
